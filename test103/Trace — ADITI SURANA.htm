Trace

Product Design, UX/UI Design

 

Overview

Trace is a mobile app that applies the idea and whimsy of “GPS Art” to keep users motivated to achieve their fitness goals. Users can draw their running patterns and share these creative traces as artworks.
 

Duration - 2 weeks  |  Role - Design Lead  |  Software - Sketch, Principle, Adobe Creative Suite

 
 
 
 

Ideation

According to a study conducted regarding the engagement with an activity tracking Android app, Habito, it was found that out of 256 users, 66% used the app for more than two days and only 14% for longer than two weeks.
Study: How Do We Engage With Activity Trackers? A Longitudinal Study of Habito; Source: ACM Digital Library

Trace attempts to offer a solution to this disengagement by deriving inspiration from the recently trending concept of GPS Art.

 

Inspiration - GPS Art

What is GPS Art?
Artists and athletes around the world are using the GPS feature of their phones and running apps like Nike+ and Strava to create beautiful artworks out of their runs.

A Portland-based copywriter and GPS artist, Claire Wyckoff, states - "Once I started running shapes and drawing pictures, I noticed that it took my mind off of running and put it in a different arena where all of a sudden my goal was no longer to huff and puff and look at my watch and roll my eyes and wish it was over, but it was actually to finish a picture, and in some cases to go and run wherever I needed to run to make that drawing work.” 

 
 
 

Concept

Trace is a mobile app that applies the idea and whimsy of “GPS Art” to keep users motivated to achieve their fitness goals. It allows the users to use the GPS feature of their phone to create patterns around them and "trace" them by running or cycling. This alternate motive to a fitness goal and eventually, sharing their creations on social media provide the positive reinforcement needed.

The app's primary focus is to make the user feel accomplished through creating and sharing their traces, which doesn't necessarily require a quantitative goal.

 

Goals

1. Create an activity app whose main focus is not fitness.
2. Allow users to convert their paths into artworks.
3. Provide motivation through playfulness, creativity and social participation.
4. Form a global community of runners, cyclists, athletes and artists.

 

Challenge / Key Question

How can art be used address the challenge of long-term engagement with a mobile app?

 
 

Research

At this initial phase, I looked at how various existing fitness apps utilize behavior models, habit formation models and playfulness to keep their users motivated. Key factors here included - gamified elements, personalization, empathy, feedback loops etc.

Spotify's Running Feature
It is interesting to note how improving technology gives rise to better motivational models. Around May 2015, Spotify ventured into fitness and launched a new feature designed for runners. The app used the phone's accelerometer and the user's listening history and preferences to find the perfect soundtrack that matched the user's running pace.

I also used running apps like Nike Run Club and Strava to better understand the context. Both the apps offer a beautiful and highly usable environment for to make their users stay motivated and achieve the set goals.

User Research

In order to establish who the target user group, I conducted informal user interviews with three participants. The goal was to understand the participant's lifestyle, goals and preferences, and see if the solution could fit into their lives.

The participants belonged to the age-group of 18-35, had a busy lifestyle and had at some point used a fitness app.

The interview helped in validating the concept and refining it further. Following the interview, I was able to formulate two sets of target users. 

The second phase of user research was to make the users go through a user task.

The task was to create pattern on the given sheet of paper with the following constraints -
1. Start from and end at the pin on the map.
2. Trace only through the white lines (streets).

Three participants were given this task. They fit the second target user group, 'Amateur Runners / Cyclists'. 

Key Questions that the user task helped analyze
1. How easily can the user create a pattern looking at their current location?
2. Does this frustrate the user?
3. How long is the user taking to finish this phase?
4. Does it affect their goal or motivation to finish the goal?
